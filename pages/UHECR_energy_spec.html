Introduction

I spent 5 years at the University of Utah conducting my Ph.D. thesis research with the Telescope Array Cosmic Ray Observatory with the help of Charles Jui (my thesis advisor) studying the Ultra-High Energy Cosmic Ray (UHECR) energy spectrum. My thesis focuses on collecting, processing, and reconstructing data of the new Telescope Array expansion (TAx4) using a hybrid analysis. Hybrid analysis utilizes information from both Surface Detector (SD) and Flourescence Detector (FD) to constrain the reconstructed shower's "Timing verse Angle" fit. This method of event reconstruction has a significantly improved resolution over monocular analyses. My analysis has yielded the first TAx4 hybrid cosmic ray energy spectrum. I also report the performance of TAx4 and present data-MC comparisions.

Abstract

Cosmic rays are subatomic particles of extraterrestrial origin and at the highest energies, they are not well

understood. The variation of the cosmic ray flux with energy is referred to as the "Energy Spectrum.‚Äù Themeasurement of the cosmic ray energy spectrum is important because it may give a hint of where cosmic rays come from. The Telescope Array(TA) Cosmic Ray Observatory, located in Millard County Utah, is the largest cosmic ray detector in the Northern hemisphere. Following evidence for a hotspot in the arrival directions of the highest energy cosmic rays, TA underwent the TAx4 upgrade to expand the area of Surface Detectors (SD) by a factor of 4. The upgrade included new Fluorescence Detector (FD) stations to view over the expanded SD arrays. In this work, I will present a preliminary cosmic ray energy spectrum using hybrid events from TAx4, the hybrid resolutions of the detector, and data-MC comparisons. I will show this work's preliminary hybrid energy spectrum is in agreement with previous TA measurements, which is a key first step towards future composition and anisotropy studies.

TAx4 Hybrid Cosmic Ray Detector

TA has found hints of anisotropy of UHECRs in the northern hemisphere. Using the TA SD data, there were 72 events found with E > 57 EeV (5.7x1019 eV) that showed a clustering of events with a 3.4 sigma significance. This clustering has been coined the hotspot. To better observe and understand the nature of the hotspot, TA is quadrupling its size. This expansion was accordingly named TAx4. 500 new Surface Detectors (SDs) are being added spread over two lopes to the north and south of the main TA array. The new SD arrays will be overlooked by 12 new Fluorescence Detector telescopes (FDs). TAx4 will increase TA's detection aperture and increase the number of UHECR events detected. One of the primary goals of TAx4 is to improve the statistical significance of the hotspot and TA's statistics overall at the highest energies. Below is map of all of TA, including TALE and TAx4. The red dots are the SD locations and overlayed on the map are photos of the FD sites.  

Map of the entire Telescope Array overlayed with pictures of TAx4 North and South.

The twelve new Fluorescence detector (FD) telescopes were added at the BR and MD FD station sites, four at MD and eight at BR. TAx4MDFD, overlooking the TAx4 northern SD array, was completed in February of 2018; after several of months of detector commissioning, regular data-taking began. TAx4BRFD, overlooking the TAx4 southern SD array, was completed in July of 2019. Detector commissioning was extended because of the need to prepare this station for remote operation from Middle Drum. This effort was further delayed by the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic and the restrictions it imposed on work. Nevertheless, regular observation did start in July, 2020.

Monte Carlo

Complex Monte Carlo (MC) simulations are used to determine the detectors responce to cosmic ray events, detector aperture, and determine detector resolutions. The MC used in this analysis was generated using QGSJetII-03 protons. The MC is constructed in such a way that it is indistingishable from the data, then the same reconstruction programs that are used on the data are used on the MC. The validity of the MC can then be determined by comparing distributions of observables produced in the data.

Hybrid Spectrum

The hybrid spectrum measured using the SDs and FDs of TAx4 using approximately 2 years of data is in reasonable agreement with the previously published TA Combined Spectrum.
